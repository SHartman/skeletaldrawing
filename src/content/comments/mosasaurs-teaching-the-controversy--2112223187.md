<p>I'm not sure that an asymmetrical hypocercal tail necessarily suggests surface swimming. It would be expected of any positively buoyant swimmer with a relatively low cruising speed (hence why multiple lineages of tail-propelled reptiles ended up with hypocercal tails. Sharks, being negatively buoyant, of course show the mirror tail morphology with heterocercal tails - as you noted). Symmetrical tails are associated with high cruising speeds, in which case it doesn't matter whether they are heterocercal or hypocercal (it just comes down to phylogenetic history at that point).</p><p>Semi-carangiform swimming will be more efficient than anguilliform swimming at essentially all body sizes, though at low speeds the efficiency difference is smaller. I suspect that the primary advantage of anguilliform swimming is not so much in the swimming itself, but rather that eel-like (or snake-like) body shapes are really useful for getting into tight spaces, etc.</p><p>The one mode where anguilliform swimming is truly mechanically advantageous isn't really a swimming mode, per se: eel-like body plans combined with a laterally flattened body make for some of the best fast-start morphologies, especially if that fast start is used for leaping (this is relatively easy to show mathematically). One would predict, for example, that a specialist aquatic jumper would have a moderately long body, laterally flattened, with a reduced caudal fin. Interestingly, this is exactly the body form of the best vertical jumpers among fish (silver arowana).</p>
