<p>I made a bit of a clarification in the article as there is (justifiable) interest here. First off, unfortunately this is the only evidence of ankle scutellae, and since they are not preserved in their entirety, their extent and size is sadly equivocal. Note that there isn't evidence from the top of the toes either. I realize that's frustrating (since this would be one of the bigger surprises) but it's all the evidence we have either way currently. There are some scales (though not scutellae) even up by the tibia (see the supplementary data), which suggests that the interplay of scales and feathers might be fairly complex in Anchiornis - possibly (though speculatively) different from what we see in living theropods.</p><p>If we're spitballing, it's certainly possible that the reason we don't have scales on the tops of the toes is because they were fluffy - but of course it's also possible that it's because of the way the slab split (or hey, maybe something like the infamous feathered and scaled chicken feet), but none of it is testable as of now.</p>
