<p>Very good review of the controversy. I agree with you that producing a skeletal reconstruction of Spinosaurus based on all these specimens is premature and might turn being misleading. As I and Chiarenza wrote ( <a href="https://peerj.com/articles/1754/" rel="nofollow ugc noopener" target="_blank">https://peerj.com/articles/...</a> ), it's wiser to restrict the name "Spinosaurus" to Stromer original skeleton from Egypt and call all other involved specimens as "Spinosaurinae indet." pending new data. My personal position is that Spinosaurus B and the 2014 specimen are related but not necessarily the same species. I keep considering their unsual limb-vertebrae proportions indicative of a clade of short-limbed spinosaurids, thus can even accept the different proportions indicate different taxa among the "Spinosaurinae clade" of short-limbed forms. <br>There is not reason to assume a priori all these specimens are a single species, and in fact I have included all of them as distinct specimens in my theropod analysis... I won't spoil anything now, but some iterations of the analysis support multiple species.</p>
