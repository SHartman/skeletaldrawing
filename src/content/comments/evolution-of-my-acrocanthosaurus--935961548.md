<p>Actually, almost no one bothers to give the hyoid any love in full body skeletal reconstructions. Chaz Knight didn't in his reconstructions of extant mammals skeletals in his anatomy for the artist book (while including other cartilaginous elements). Bakker added in cartilaginous sternal elements in his skeletals, but skipped on the sternal ribs and the hyoid apparatus. Scheele was inconsistent in his, but mostly avoided cartilage (and often preserved sternal plates as well).<br><br>So in a way the trend of leaving out hyoids while adding in some amount of cartilage-based osteology is a long tradition. But yes, in essence Greg did indeed solidify that trend, and there's no reason to attack it on those grounds. Greg put out the largest body of work when few others were doing it in any sort of systemtized way, and as such he had a "founder effect" on what the current radiation looks like in the "skeletal drawing ecosystem". Attacking an idea on the fact that he was in the right place at the right time doesn't do anyone any good, and can lead to more fragmenting of what standards there are.<br><br>We're much better off trying to reason out improvements and adopting them via consensus, rather than simply laughing off parts we don't like.</p>
