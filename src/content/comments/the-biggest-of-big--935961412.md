Either way they are going to be close enough that we really have no idea which species got larger (given the utter lack of knowledge of wear these individual specimens should end up on their respective bell curves).<br><br>I don't really believe other people's cross-scaling until I check it, so I'll have to abstain from commenting on the tail of Phuwiangosaurus for the time being. Also I'm pretty skeptical of the more derived position that Currie-Rodgers recovered for Argentinosaurus in the 2005 paper. Mind you I'm not arguing that all derived titanosaurs would have long tails, just that somewhere at the base of the tree those tails should be shorter.<br><br>I have a post coming up about the ridiculously large Alamosaurus tibia, and I actually got closer to 30 meters, although you and I restore somewhat different limb proportions. Your mass estimates are about what I've been guestimating, and it looks to me like that 60-to-say-75 ton range may be the top for very large titanosaurs (at least those known).<br><br>Thanks again for the thoughtful commentss; there's more to come! </p>
