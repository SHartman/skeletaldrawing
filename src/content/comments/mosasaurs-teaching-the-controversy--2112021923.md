<p>Well, as mosasaurids are presumably surface hunters, then a hypocercal adaptation of the tail would suit them well (assuming ALL of them are surface hunters). The morphology of caudal fins can often speak a bit about an animal's preferred ecological marine niche (Mako Sharks have caudal fins of equal length as an adaptation towards speed, mesopelagic sharks have the dorsal caudal fin much larger than the ventral caudal fin, etc,..), though I'm not sure if this holds true for ALL marine vertebrates.<br>.......Then again, a mosasaur swimming with gentle lateral undulations of the body like a shark seems more beneficial to such a large animal rather than eel-like methods of swimming (with simple, paddle-shaped tail flukes). But don't modern squamates swim with an eel-like motion when they enter the water yeah? Is semi-carangiform anatomy really more beneficial to mosasaurs than the typical lateral-swishing that modern squamates do? </p>
