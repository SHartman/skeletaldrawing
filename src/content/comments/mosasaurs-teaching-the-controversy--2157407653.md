<p>I think you're spot on, actually. Reversed heterocercal tails also occur in the earliest members of Ichthyosauria and Archaeoceti (Lindgren et al 2010). I think a complex, completely laterally compressed body morphology, which is not efficient in very large vertebrates, is where you'd expect to see anguilliform style swimming.  Notice how in small fish, they often utilize an anguilliform or semi-carangiform swimming style, but the larger the fish get, the more they use carangiform and thunniform swimming?</p><p>-Reference :</p><p> Lindgren J, Caldwell MW, Konishi T, Chiappe LM (2010) Convergent <br>Evolution in Aquatic Tetrapods: Insights from an Exceptional Fossil <br>Mosasaur. PLoS ONE 5(8): e11998. doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0011998</p>
