In general proportions, the anatomy of _Diplodocus hallorum_ is not that different from _Diplodocus carnegii._ But the history of the name is significantly more complicated. In 1878 when _Diplodocus_ was first named, it was given the species name _D. longus_, owing to how long the tail was. Some other remains were put into _D. longus_, but more recently it has been argued that the scrappy original remains of _D. longus_ were indeterminate - that is, you couldn't tell what species it was. 

Meanwhile, in 1979 a large specimen of _Diplodocus_ was discovered in New Mexico. This specimen (NMMNH 3690) was originally given a new name - _Seismosaurus hallorum._ It took a long time to extract the NMMNH specimen from the hard sandstone that encased it, and in the meantime outlandish estimates were produced, claiming the specimen could be up to 45 meters (150 feet) long and potentially over 100 tonnes in weight. Now that the specimen is described and available to be measured, it appears to be closer to 30 meters, and was lightly built like other species of _Diplodocus_. So much so that multiple authors sunk _Seismosaurus_ into _Diplodocus_, creating the new species _Diplodocus hallorum_. 

Confused? It gets better - phylogenetic studies looking at individual _Diplodocus_ specimens have shown that many of the specimens once referred to _D. longus_ group with _D. hallorum_ - so now _D. hallorum_ is represented by the large NMMNH 3690, and several "normal-sized" specimens, including AMNH 223. Both of these specimens are shown here with their known material, rather than full reconstructions.
