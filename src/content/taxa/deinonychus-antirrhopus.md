_Deinonychus_ is somehow both more and less famous than perhaps it should be. From the vantage point of today, we can see it as a typical dromaeosaur from the Early Cretaceous of the western United States. At up to 3.5 meters (11 feet) and the weight of a human (70 kg or 150 pounds), this winged hunter would be large enough to scare potential time travelers, but it was smaller than larger dromaeosaurs like _Utahraptor_ and _Achillobator_. But it should probably be more famous, since its discovery in the 1960s lit the flames that started the [Dinosaur Renaissance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dinosaur_renaissance), the era from the late 1960s to the mid-1990s when prior assumptions about dinosaurs as cold-blooded, lizard-like animals were overturned, and we realized birds were a type of living dinosaur.

John Ostrom's work on _Deinonychus_ seemed to him incompatible with prior assumptions, and he was shocked at how much _Deinonychus_ had in common with _Archaeopteryx_, producing papers that helped inspire the broader era of debate and investigation. So why isn't _Deinonychus_ more famous than some of its relatives? When Michael Crichton wrote the book Jurassic Park, he famously adopted a suggestion by Greg Paul that _Deinonychus_ could be subsumed into the genus _Velociraptor_...and even though the idea never caught on scientifically, Crichton felt the nickname "raptor" was much cooler and befit the animal antagonists of his book. Steven Spielberg adopted "raptor" for the Jurassic Park movies, and _Deinonychus_ as a public celebrity was lost, despite it being the actual star of the story.

Yet too much can also be made out of _Deinonychus_. While _Deinonychus_ fossils aren't terribly rare, they lack well-preserved, articulated individuals, so reconstructions have to be composites of multiple specimens. Despite that, we know they were like other dromaeosaurs in having well-developed, clawed wings, enlarged claws on their hyper-extendable second toes, which they may have used to hold down struggling prey while starting to eat, and a large, long head full of teeth.
