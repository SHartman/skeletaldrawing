Last week saw the publication of *Teleocrater.* A new species (and a new group) of Triassic archosaurs is always exciting, but this shed some much-needed light on the split between dinosaurs and crocodilians. But the science isn't the only story - the paper is also the heartwarming conclusion of a 70 year journey for the fossils post-collection, and for the man who first described them.

\[caption id="" align="alignnone" width="250"\]![ Alan Charig with Udanoceratops skull. Photo from here . ](/images/blog/teleocrater-2.webp) Alan Charig with Udanoceratops skull. Photo from here . \[/caption\]

In 1956, British paleontologist [Alan Charig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alan_J._Charig) finished his doctoral dissertation on Triassic fossils from what is now Tanzania. In it he informally named some of the new animals, including *Teleocrater*. *Teleocrater* means "complete basin", and is in reference to the hip socket\* being solid (like yours, or crocodiles) instead of open at the back like dinosaurs. This feature was presumably something of a surprise to Charig, as the rest of the skeleton looked like something you would see in an early dinosaur (or close dinosaur relative).

Dr. Charig had a long and celebrated career, but he never got around to formally publishing his work on *Teleocrater.* This left it in taxonomic limbo - everyone knew the name (it's had a [Wikipedia page](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teleocrater) since 2006) but until it was published it in a qualifying outlet an unscrupulous person could publish a different name at any point.

In a demonstration that not all science is cutthroat, no one claim-jumped *Teleocrater*, and last week [a paper by Nesbitt et al.](http://www.nature.com/nature/journal/vaop/ncurrent/full/nature22037.html) (which fittingly included Charig in the author list) officially named *Teleocrater rhadinus*.

But what over the weird combination of croc-like hip socket and dinosaur-like vertebrate? It got weirder - the authors found that the hind leg was also croc-like, especially the foot and ankle. The animal appears to have spent its life on all four limbs, unlike the usually bipedal ancestors of dinosaurs. Yet there were a large number of characters from the skull and vertebrae that showed it really does belong at the base of the dinosaur branch of the archosaur tree rather than the croc-branch.

This means that despite some superficial similarity to crocodile ancestors, *Teleocrater* is actually a great-great-great granduncle of birds (add a few million more "greats" in there for full effect). Even better, several other Middle and Late Triassic archosaurs turn out to be close relatives of *Teleocrater*, who are all now grouped together in the [Aphanosauria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aphanosauria). So now we have a widely-distributed family of basal dinosaur relatives, and they show that when the first split between dinosaurs and crocs happened, the critters involved looked a lot more like long-necked crocs than like dinosaurs.

\*The "hip socket" is technically referred to as an acetabulum. So now you know your acetabulum from a hole in the ground.
